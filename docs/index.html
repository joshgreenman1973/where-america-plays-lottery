Methodology, sources and what this does not show

The big caveat, up front. These are sales recorded at the store, not the buyer's home. A ZIP code near offices, highways or a state line will shade dark because of commuters and cross-border shoppers, not residents. Per-resident figures in commercial ZIP codes are inflated for this reason and should not be read as how much locals spend.

    Why only New York and Texas? Of the roughly 45 states with lotteries, these are the only two that publish current, machine-readable sales broken out by retailer location. Pennsylvania publishes county totals but stopped updating in 2022; Massachusetts releases town totals as reports; most states release only a list of where you can buy, not how much sells. A uniform national map of lottery sales by neighborhood is not possible because the data does not exist.

    Sources: New York State Gaming Commission — daily retailer sales; Texas Lottery Commission — sales by retailer; population from the U.S. Census Bureau American Community Survey 2019–2023 five-year estimates (total population by ZIP code tabulation area); ZIP boundaries from the Census 2020 cartographic files, simplified.

    How this was built — and its limits

    This page was assembled with AI assistance. Before relying on it, know:
    • Sales are by store location, not buyer residence — see the methodology note.
    • New York and Texas report sales differently; the two are made comparable but not identical.
    • Figures are unaudited as published by each state and cover a trailing 12-month window.
    • Shading uses within-view quantile bins, so colors rescale when you switch states or metric.
    • No claim is made about who plays or why — only where tickets are sold.
